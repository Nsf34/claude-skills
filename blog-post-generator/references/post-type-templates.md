## 1. Buying Guide

A comprehensive guide that helps the reader evaluate options in a product category. Positions the user's product within the landscape without reading like a product page.

### Dossier Section Mapping

| Blog Section | Primary Source | Secondary Source |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Introduction (why this category matters) | S5: VoC motivations | S3: Market Landscape trends |
| What to Look For (buying criteria) | S3: Needs-to-category mapping | S4: Competitor strengths (what good looks like) |
| Product Comparison Table | S4: Competitor table | S10: Pricing data |
| Individual Product Spotlights | S4: Competitor gaps/strengths | S9: Messaging (for the user's product) |
| Customer Perspectives | S2: Verbatim quotes | S5: VoC themed quotes |
| Price Breakdown | S10: Pricing & bundling | S4: Competitor pricing column |
| Common Questions | S6: Objection Kill Sheet | S5: VoC objections |
| Conclusion & Recommendation | S9: Core promise | S8: Persona-specific messaging |

### Structural Template

```
# [Category] Buying Guide: [Compelling, Keyword-Rich Title]

## Why [Category] Is Worth Considering
[Frame the reader's need using VoC motivations from S5. What problem
does this category solve? Why are people looking? Include 1-2 market
trend insights from S3 to establish the category is growing/relevant.]

## What to Look For in a [Product Type]
[Turn the needs-to-category mapping from S3 into practical buying criteria.
Each criterion becomes an H3 subsection with 2-3 sentences explaining why
it matters. Use competitor strengths from S4 to illustrate what "good"
looks like for each criterion.]

### [Criterion 1: e.g., Ease of Setup]
### [Criterion 2: e.g., What's Included]
### [Criterion 3: e.g., Learning Resources]
### [Criterion 4: e.g., Material Quality]

## [N] Best [Product Types] Compared
[Comparison table drawn from S4 competitor table. Include 4-6 most
relevant competitors + the user's product. Columns: Product, Price,
Key Features, Best For, Our Take.]

### [User's Product Name]
[Lead with this. Use S4 competitive gaps to position it favorably.
Ground claims in dossier research. Include 1-2 customer quotes from S2.]

### [Competitor 2]
[Fair assessment from S4 — strengths and limitations.]

### [Competitor 3]
[Same treatment — balanced, research-grounded.]

## What Real Customers Say
[3-4 verbatim quotes from S2, selected for thematic diversity. Each
quote should do work: illustrate ease of use, validate quality,
address a common concern, or capture the emotional payoff.]

## Common Questions About [Category]
[3-5 FAQ entries drawn from S6 Objection Kill Sheet. Frame each
objection as a natural question. Use the dossier's rebuttal strategy
adapted to conversational blog tone. Include supporting customer
quotes where available.]

## The Bottom Line
[Balanced conclusion that naturally positions the user's product.
Use the core promise from S9. Tie back to the reader's initial need
from the introduction. Include a soft CTA.]
```

### Tone
Informational, balanced, genuinely helpful. The user's product should be featured prominently but the guide should feel useful to someone honestly researching the category. Think "helpful friend who's done the research" not "brand spokesperson."

### Word Count
2,000–3,000 words

### Quote Usage
4-6 quotes, spread across Customer Perspectives section and sprinkled in product spotlights.

## 2. Problem-Solution

Opens with a pain point the reader recognizes, agitates it with specific evidence, then introduces the product category and specific product as the resolution.

### Dossier Section Mapping

| Blog Section | Primary Source | Secondary Source |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| The Problem (hook) | S5: VoC pain points + objections | S6: Top objections as symptom evidence |
| Why It's Harder Than It Looks | S6: Objection root causes | S4: Competitor limitations |
| The Solution Exists | S3: Market Landscape | S5: VoC motivations (what drove purchase) |
| How [Product] Solves It | S9: Messaging hierarchy | S6: Rebuttal strategies as proof |
| Proof It Works | S2: Verbatim quotes | S5: VoC success stories |
| Who It's Best For | S8: Persona profiles | S5: Motivation clusters |
| Conclusion + CTA | S9: Core promise | S8: Persona-specific messaging |

### Structural Template

```
# [Problem Statement as Title — e.g., "Why Most Beginner Pottery
Kits End Up in the Closet (And What Actually Works)"]

## The [Problem] Nobody Talks About
[Open with the core pain point using customer language from S5.
Make the reader feel seen — this section should read like you've
been in their shoes. Use 1-2 short quotes that capture the
frustration.]

## Why [Obvious Solutions] Fall Short
[Draw from S4 competitor limitations and S6 objection root causes.
Show that the reader's past failures aren't their fault — existing
options have structural problems. Be specific about what goes wrong
and why.]

## What Actually Works (and Why)
[Transition from problem to solution category using S3 market context.
What has changed? What do successful approaches have in common? Use
VoC motivation data from S5 to ground this in what real buyers
discovered.]

## How [Product] Approaches This Differently
[Use S9 messaging hierarchy — lead with the core promise, support
with specific features. Address 2-3 top objections from S6 inline
using the rebuttal strategies. This section should feel like evidence,
not marketing.]

## What People Are Saying
[3-4 verbatim quotes from S2, specifically chosen for transformation
narratives — "I expected X but got Y." Include quotes that address
the specific problem from the opening.]

## Is This Right for You?
[Draw from S8 persona profiles. Describe 2-3 ideal user profiles
without using the word "persona." Frame as "this works especially
well if you..." and "you might want something different if..."]

## Getting Started
[Brief, encouraging conclusion. Use the core promise from S9.
Soft CTA that matches the post's informational tone.]
```

### Tone
Empathetic and authoritative. The reader came here with a problem — respect that. No hype. Show you understand the frustration, then demonstrate the solution with evidence.

### Word Count
1,500–2,500 words

### Quote Usage
3-5 quotes. Strongest impact when placed after making a claim — the quote serves as immediate proof.

## 3. Comparison Post

Direct head-to-head comparison of 2-4 products. Drives decision-stage traffic — readers searching "[Product A] vs [Product B]."

### Dossier Section Mapping

| Blog Section | Primary Source | Secondary Source |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Introduction (what we're comparing) | S4: Competitor overview | S3: Category context |
| Quick Comparison Table | S4: Competitor table | S10: Pricing |
| Category-by-Category Breakdown | S4: Strengths/gaps per competitor | S5: What customers compare |
| What Customers Say About Each | S2 + S5: Quotes mentioning competitors | S6: Product-specific objections |
| Who Should Choose What | S8: Personas | S5: Motivation-to-product mapping |
| Price & Value Analysis | S10: Pricing analysis | S4: Bundle contents |
| Final Verdict | S4: Competitive gaps | S9: Positioning |

### Structural Template

```
# [Product A] vs [Product B] vs [Product C]: [Year] Comparison

## What We're Comparing (and Why)
[Brief intro establishing why these products are commonly compared.
Use S3 to set the category context and S4 to explain what makes
these the top contenders.]

## At a Glance
[Side-by-side comparison table. Columns: Feature, Product A,
Product B, Product C. Rows: Price, What's Included, Best For,
Key Strength, Key Limitation. All data from S4 + S10.]

## [Comparison Dimension 1: e.g., What You Get in the Box]
[S4 strengths/gaps for each product on this dimension. Include
a relevant customer quote if available.]

## [Comparison Dimension 2: e.g., Learning Curve]
[Same structure — research-grounded comparison.]

## [Comparison Dimension 3: e.g., Quality & Durability]
[Same structure.]

## [Comparison Dimension 4: e.g., Value for Money]
[S10 pricing analysis applied to each product.]

## What Actual Buyers Say
[2-3 quotes per product from S2/S5, organized by product.
Show the authentic customer perspective for each option.]

## Which One Is Right for You?
[Use S8 personas to create "Choose X if you..." recommendations.
Each product gets matched to a persona profile.]

## The Bottom Line
[Fair summary. Acknowledge each product's strengths. Position the
user's product based on genuine competitive advantages from S4.]
```

### Tone
Balanced and fair. The comparison must feel honest — readers will bounce immediately if it reads like a rigged review. Give competitors genuine credit where the dossier supports it. Win on merit, not spin.

### Word Count
2,000–3,000 words

### Quote Usage
4-6 quotes, distributed across products (not all on the user's product).

## 4. Why [Category] Educational

Educational content that builds awareness of and interest in a product category. Targets top-of-funnel readers who may not be actively shopping yet.

### Dossier Section Mapping

| Blog Section | Primary Source | Secondary Source |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Introduction (category overview) | S3: Market Landscape | S1: Executive Summary |
| The Rise of [Category] | S3: Market trends | S5: VoC motivations (why people are drawn to this) |
| Benefits (what the research shows) | S5: VoC motivations by theme | S2: Quotes about benefits |
| Who's Getting Into [Category] | S8: Persona profiles | S5: Motivation clusters |
| Common Misconceptions | S6: Assumption-based objections | S5: VoC objections |
| How to Get Started | S3: Needs mapping | S4: Entry-level options |
| Conclusion | S9: Core messaging | S1: Key takeaway |

### Structural Template

```
# Why [Category] Is [Compelling Claim — e.g., "The Creative Outlet
You Didn't Know You Needed"]

## [Category] Is Having a Moment
[Set the scene with S3 market trends. What's driving interest?
Cultural shifts, lifestyle changes, social media trends. Ground
in research, not speculation.]

## [N] Reasons People Are [Doing This]
[Each reason is an H3 drawn from S5 VoC motivation themes. Use
customer language to make each reason feel real. Include 1 quote
per reason where available.]

### [Reason 1: e.g., It's Genuinely Relaxing]
### [Reason 2: e.g., You Make Something Real]
### [Reason 3: e.g., It's Surprisingly Social]
### [Reason 4: e.g., Anyone Can Start]

## Who's Actually Doing This?
[Draw from S8 personas but frame as broad audience segments.
"Busy parents looking for screen-free family time" not
"Persona 3: The Overwhelmed Parent." Make the reader see
themselves.]

## "But Isn't It [Objection]?"
[Address 3-4 common misconceptions from S6, specifically the
assumption-based objections. Frame as myths, address with evidence
from the dossier's rebuttal strategies.]

## How to Get Started (Without Overthinking It)
[Use S3 needs mapping to identify what a beginner actually needs.
Reference S4 entry-level options. Keep it practical and encouraging.]

## The Takeaway
[Brief conclusion. Use S9 core messaging adapted for educational
tone — less "buy this" and more "this is worth exploring."]
```

### Tone
Curious, warm, educational. This post is for someone who doesn't know they want this yet. No selling — just genuinely interesting content about the category.

### Word Count
1,500–2,000 words

### Quote Usage
3-4 quotes, selected for "discovery moment" energy — quotes that capture surprise, delight, or unexpected benefits.

## 5. Gift Guide

Positions the product as a gift for specific recipients and occasions. High seasonal value (holidays, birthdays, Mother's/Father's Day).

### Dossier Section Mapping

| Blog Section | Primary Source | Secondary Source |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Introduction (gift context) | S7: Contextual Positioning (gift angles) | S5: VoC gift-related motivations |
| Why [Product] Makes a Great Gift | S7: Gift hooks | S2: Gift-related quotes |
| Best For [Recipient Types] | S8: Personas (as gift recipients) | S7: Occasion mapping |
| What's Included (unboxing angle) | S4: Bundle contents (user's product) | S10: Bundle tiers |
| Gift Buying Guide (by tier) | S10: Bundle/tier recommendations | S7: Price-occasion matching |
| What Gift-Givers Are Saying | S2 + S5: Gift-specific quotes | S7: Gift success evidence |
| FAQ for Gift Buyers | S6: Gift-specific objections | S7: Hesitation points |
| Conclusion + CTA | S9: Gift-angle messaging | S7: Strongest hook |

### Structural Template

```
# [Product] Gift Guide: The [Compelling Promise — e.g., "Gift That
Actually Gets Used"]

## Why [Category] Gifts Are Different
[Frame the gift-giving problem: most gifts are forgotten. Use S7
contextual positioning and S5 gift motivations to explain why this
category breaks the pattern.]

## Who This Is Perfect For
[2-4 recipient profiles drawn from S8 personas, reframed as people
the reader might be shopping for. "The person who has everything,"
"The creative one who never makes time for themselves," etc.]

### [Recipient Type 1]
### [Recipient Type 2]
### [Recipient Type 3]

## What They'll Actually Get
[Unboxing angle. Use S4 bundle contents for the user's product.
Describe the experience of receiving it, not just the components.
If S5 has unboxing-related quotes, include one.]

## Gift Options by Budget
[Use S10 tier recommendations. Frame each tier as an occasion match:
entry = stocking stuffer / add-on gift, core = standalone gift,
premium = the "wow" gift.]

## What Other Gift-Givers Say
[3-4 gift-specific quotes from S2/S5. Focus on reactions: "My
daughter's face when she opened it...", "Best gift I've ever
given..." These are emotional proof points.]

## Gift Buyer FAQ
[2-3 gift-specific concerns from S6/S7: "Will they actually use it?",
"What if they have no experience?", "Is it appropriate for a kid?"]

## Ready to Order?
[Brief, seasonal-aware CTA. If S7 has timing data (shipping
cutoffs, holiday relevance), include it.]
```

### Tone
Warm, helpful, reassuring. The reader is making a choice for someone else — they need confidence that this gift will land well. Focus on the recipient's experience, not product specs.

### Word Count
1,500–2,000 words

### Quote Usage
3-5 quotes, all gift-specific. If the dossier doesn't have enough gift-focused quotes, note this in the Research Map and pull from adjacent VoC themes.

### Important Note
This post type depends heavily on S7 (Contextual Positioning). If S7 is thin or absent in the dossier, flag it to the user and suggest either running additional gift-focused research or pivoting to a different post type.

## 6. FAQ / Myth-Busting

Directly addresses the objections and concerns that prevent purchase. High SEO value because people literally search these questions.

### Dossier Section Mapping

| Blog Section | Primary Source | Secondary Source |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Introduction | S5: VoC objection themes | S1: Key objection finding |
| Each FAQ Entry | S6: Objection Kill Sheet (1 entry per FAQ) | S5: VoC quotes for that objection |
| Quick Facts Summary | S3: Market data | S4: Competitive context |
| Conclusion | S6: Friction-reduction synthesis | S9: Core promise |

### Structural Template

```
# [N] Common Questions About [Product/Category] — Answered Honestly

## Why These Questions Matter
[Brief intro acknowledging that these concerns are real and
legitimate. Use S5 to show how common they are. Set the tone:
honest answers, not corporate deflection.]

## [FAQ 1: "Is it too [objection]?"]
[Draw from S6 Objection Kill Sheet entry. Structure:
1. Acknowledge the concern is valid (use customer language from S5)
2. Explain the root cause (from S6 "Cause" field)
3. Present the evidence-based answer (from S6 "Rebuttal Strategy")
4. Include a customer quote that resolved this concern
End with a practical takeaway.]

## [FAQ 2: "What if [objection]?"]
[Same structure. Each FAQ is one Objection Kill Sheet entry
reformatted for a conversational blog voice.]

## [FAQ 3: "Is it worth the [price/effort]?"]
## [FAQ 4: "How does it compare to [alternative]?"]
## [FAQ 5: "Do I need [prerequisite]?"]
## [FAQ 6: "What about [specific concern]?"]

[Continue for 6-10 FAQ entries, prioritized by frequency data
from S5/S6.]

## Quick Facts
[3-5 bullet points with objective data from S3/S4: market context,
what the product includes, price range.]

## The Short Version
[2-3 sentence conclusion. Acknowledge remaining concerns are
normal. Point the reader toward next steps — not a hard sell,
but "if the answers above addressed your main concerns, here's
where to learn more."]
```

### Tone
Honest and direct. These readers are skeptical — they came here specifically because they have doubts. Respect that by being transparent. If a concern has merit (e.g., "it is messy"), acknowledge it and explain the mitigation rather than pretending the concern doesn't exist.

### Word Count
1,500–2,000 words

### Quote Usage
1 quote per FAQ entry where available (6-10 total). Each quote should demonstrate that a real customer had this concern and resolved it.

## 7. Customer Story / Use Case

Narrative content built from real customer voices. Not a fabricated case study — a composite picture drawn from authentic quotes and persona data.

### Dossier Section Mapping

| Blog Section | Primary Source | Secondary Source |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Introduction (the shared experience) | S5: Dominant VoC motivation theme | S8: Target persona profile |
| The Problem Before | S5: Pain points | S6: Objection causes |
| The Discovery | S5: How customers found the product | S3: Where this sits in the market |
| The Experience | S2: Positive experience quotes | S5: VoC success themes |
| The Unexpected Benefits | S5: Surprise motivations | S2: Quotes about unexpected value |
| Who Else This Works For | S8: Other persona profiles | S7: Contextual angles |
| Conclusion | S2: Strongest transformation quote | S9: Core promise |

### Structural Template

```
# [Title Framing the Transformation — e.g., "From Stress Relief
to Side Business: How People Are Using [Product]"]

## A Story You Might Recognize
[Open with a composite scenario built from S5 and S8. Frame a
relatable situation the target persona would recognize. Use S5
language patterns — the exact words customers use to describe
their pre-purchase state.]

**Important: State clearly that this represents a composite of
real customer experiences, not a single individual.**

## The [Problem] That Started It
[Draw from S5 pain points and S6 objection causes. What was the
reader's life like before? What weren't they getting from existing
solutions? Use 1-2 frustration quotes.]

## Finding [Product/Category]
[Transition using S5 discovery motivations. How do real customers
describe finding this? Was it a gift? A social media discovery?
A recommendation?]

## What It's Actually Like
[Build from S2 positive quotes and S5 success themes. Let customer
voices carry this section. Interweave 3-4 quotes with brief
connective narrative. This should feel like a chorus of real voices.]

## The Things Nobody Expected
[Draw from S5 surprise motivations — benefits customers didn't
anticipate. "I bought it for stress relief but now I sell pieces
at craft fairs." These unexpected outcomes are the most
compelling content.]

## Other Ways People Use It
[Use S8 persona profiles and S7 contextual positioning to show
breadth. "Parents use it as screen-free family time. Therapists
recommend it to clients. Office workers use it as a decompression
ritual." Each angle from dossier data.]

## The Common Thread
[Brief conclusion weaving together the voices. Use the single
strongest transformation quote from S2 as the closing beat.
Soft CTA.]
```

### Tone
Warm, narrative, human. This post should feel like reading about real people, not marketing personas. Let the quotes do the heavy lifting. The writer's job is to weave them into a coherent, emotionally resonant story.

### Word Count
1,200–1,800 words

### Quote Usage
6-8 quotes minimum. This is the most quote-heavy post type. Select for narrative arc — frustration → discovery → experience → transformation.

### Critical Note
Be transparent about the composite nature of the content. A line like "The experiences below are drawn from real customer reviews across Amazon, Reddit, and social media" establishes credibility without misleading.

## 8. Listicle / X Reasons

Highly scannable, shareable format. Each "reason" is a self-contained argument supported by research.

### Dossier Section Mapping

| Blog Section | Primary Source | Secondary Source |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Introduction | S5: VoC top motivation | S1: Executive Summary hook |
| Each Reason | S9: Ad angles (1 angle per reason) | S5: Supporting VoC data |
| Evidence Blocks | S2: Verbatim quotes | S4: Competitive gaps (differentiation) |
| Conclusion | S9: Messaging hierarchy | S8: Persona-specific messaging |

### Structural Template

```
# [N] Reasons [Product/Category] Is [Compelling Claim]

## Why This List Exists
[2-3 sentences framing the post. Use S5 top motivation to establish
why readers should care. Keep it short — listicle readers want to
get to the list.]

## 1. [Reason — adapted from S9 Ad Angle]
[150-250 words. Open with the hook from the ad angle concept.
Support with VoC data from S5 — what do real customers say about
this benefit? Include 1 quote if available. Close with the specific
evidence that makes this reason credible.]

## 2. [Reason]
[Same structure. Each reason should stand alone — a reader who
skips here from scanning should get a complete argument.]

## 3. [Reason]
## 4. [Reason]
## 5. [Reason]
## 6. [Reason]
## 7. [Reason]

[Continue for 7-12 reasons. Adapt from S9 ad angles, supplemented
by VoC motivations from S5 and competitive gaps from S4. Vary the
type of evidence across reasons — some quote-driven, some
data-driven, some comparison-driven.]

## The Bigger Picture
[Brief conclusion that ties the individual reasons into a coherent
message. Use S9 messaging hierarchy — the reasons are the
supporting bullets, the conclusion delivers the core promise.]
```

### Tone
Energetic, confident, evidence-backed. Listicles need momentum — each reason should feel like a satisfying "hit" of information. But every claim still needs research grounding. Think "punchy with substance" not "clickbait with nothing behind it."

### Word Count
1,500–2,500 words

### Quote Usage
3-5 quotes, distributed across reasons (not every reason needs one). Place quotes where they add the most credibility — especially for reasons that might seem like marketing claims without proof.

### Structure Note
The optimal number of reasons depends on the dossier's strength. If S9 has 15 ad angles, you might do "12 Reasons." If it has 8, do "7 Reasons." Don't pad the list with weak entries — each reason should carry real weight.
